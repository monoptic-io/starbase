# Harmony

**Harmony** is what happens when [[Chord|chords]] move. A single chord just *is*; harmony is the *story* chords tell as they follow one another, leaning away from a home base and then falling back into it. That push and pull — tension built, tension released — is the engine that drives almost all music forward.

## Home, away, and back: I–IV–V–I

Number the chords built on each degree of a [[Scale]] with Roman numerals: the chord on the first note is **I** (the *tonic*, home), on the fourth **IV** (the *subdominant*), on the fifth **V** (the *dominant*). The most fundamental progression in Western music is

$$\text{I} \rightarrow \text{IV} \rightarrow \text{V} \rightarrow \text{I}.$$

In C major that is C → F → G → C. The **V** chord is the moment of greatest tension: it contains the *leading tone*, a note just one semitone below home that practically begs to resolve upward. When **V** finally falls to **I**, that satisfying click of arrival is called a **cadence** — the musical equivalent of a sentence's final period.

The bars trace the arc: we leave home (I), drift (IV), strain at the dominant (V), then snap back to rest (I). Reorder the same chords and you get a different feeling entirely — harmony is as much about *sequence* as about the chords themselves.

{{< note kind="key" title="Tension and resolution" >}}
Nearly every harmonic effect reduces to one idea: set up an expectation, then satisfy or delay it. A [[Chord]] that contains a tense [[Interval]] *wants* to move; harmony is the art of choosing when to let it.
{{< /note >}}

## Why fifths feel like motion

The strongest, most "inevitable" chord moves are by a fifth — exactly the relationship mapped by the [[Circle of Fifths]]. V→I is a fall of a fifth, and chaining such falls (vi→ii→V→I) produces the smooth, tumbling-forward momentum heard in countless songs.
