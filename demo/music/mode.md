# Mode

A **mode** is what you get when you take one [[Scale]] and start it on a different note. Play only the white keys but treat **D** as home instead of C, and the *same seven pitches* suddenly sound minor-ish and folky. You have not changed the notes — you have changed which one feels like the center, and that single shift rewrites the pattern of [[Interval|intervals]] around home. The major scale has seven notes, so it hides **seven modes**.

## The seven rotations

Each mode is the major-scale steps rotated to begin on a different degree. Their classical Greek names and characteristic moods:

| Mode | Start on (in C) | Flavor |
|---|---|---|
| Ionian | C | the plain **major** scale — bright, resolved |
| Dorian | D | minor, but hopeful; jazzy |
| Phrygian | E | dark, Spanish, tense |
| Lydian | F | dreamy, floating (raised 4th) |
| Mixolydian | G | major with a bluesy lowered 7th |
| Aeolian | A | the **natural minor** scale — wistful |
| Locrian | B | unstable, rarely used (diminished home chord) |

Two of these you already know under other names: **Ionian** *is* the major scale and **Aeolian** *is* the natural minor. The other five are the same white keys reheard from a new home.

{{< note kind="key" title="Same notes, different center" >}}
A mode proves that music is about *relationships*, not absolute pitches. Move the tonal center and the very same collection of notes — the same [[Key Signature]] — tells a completely different emotional story.
{{< /note >}}

## Why modes feel different

The mood lives in *where the half-steps fall relative to home*. In Ionian the half-steps sit between degrees 3–4 and 7–8, giving that strong pull home. Shift the start and those half-steps land in new places: Phrygian puts one right above the tonic (a tense lowered second), which is exactly why it sounds so dark. The brightness or darkness of a mode lines up neatly with its position on the [[Circle of Fifths]] — brighter modes have more sharps, darker ones more flats.
